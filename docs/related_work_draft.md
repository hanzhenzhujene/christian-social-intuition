# Related Work Draft

Research on LLM morality has developed quickly, but much of it still evaluates moral behavior through a single output that combines verdict and rationale. That literature is directly relevant to our setting, yet it leaves open the mechanism question that motivates the present paper. We therefore situate this work at the intersection of three lines of research: LLM moral reasoning benchmarks, prompt-framing and persona effects, and moral-psychological theories of intuition and post-hoc justification.

## LLM Moral Reasoning Benchmarks

One important background line is the growing literature on evaluating LLMs with moral dilemmas, social scenarios, and ethics-oriented judgment tasks [LLM moral prompting]. This work has shown that language models are sensitive to scenario wording, model family, prompting strategy, and evaluation metric. Some papers focus on canonical philosophical dilemmas, including trolley-style tradeoffs or harm-minimization judgments, while others use broader scenario collections aimed at more naturalistic moral reasoning. The Moral Stories benchmark is especially relevant for the present study because it emphasizes everyday social situations involving norms, intentions, and consequences rather than only sacrificial dilemma templates [Moral Stories]. That makes it a better substrate for testing motive-sensitive framing effects, since the scenarios leave room for distinctions between outward behavior, underlying intention, and downstream interpersonal harm.

At the same time, most LLM morality benchmarks are optimized for broad comparison rather than mechanism isolation. They typically ask the model for one answer, sometimes with a justification, and then score the resulting output for correctness, alignment with human labels, or closeness to an expected norm. This design has obvious practical advantages: it is efficient, easy to scale, and amenable to leaderboard-style comparison. But it also creates an interpretive limitation. If judgment and explanation are elicited together, a framing manipulation that changes only the explanation can look similar to one that changes the exposed moral judgment itself. Our paper builds on the benchmark tradition while departing from its usual response format. Instead of treating a single moral response as a unitary endpoint, we treat it as a staged sequence with potentially different causal sensitivities.

This difference is important methodologically. Existing benchmark work has done a great deal to show that models can produce recognizable moral judgments and that those judgments can vary across prompts and model families. What it has done much less often is separate first-pass exposed choice from later justification within the same experimental design. The present paper therefore complements rather than replaces benchmark-style work. We retain the benchmark grounding of everyday moral scenarios, but reconfigure the response structure to ask a narrower question about what prompt manipulations are actually moving.

## Prompt Framing, Persona, and Value Conditioning

The second relevant line of work examines how LLM outputs change under prompting interventions such as personas, role instructions, value framing, constitutional rules, or broader steering cues [persona prompting; value-conditioned prompting; AI alignment and values]. This literature has established that language models can be highly responsive to framing. A model instructed to speak as a certain profession, ideology, or social role may shift not only style but also substantive choices. Similarly, value-conditioned prompting can alter the kinds of reasons a model foregrounds, the tradeoffs it emphasizes, or the level of caution it adopts. These findings matter for the present paper because Christian heart-focused framing is, at one level, another instance of a value- or persona-adjacent intervention.

However, prompt-framing research often optimizes for the existence and magnitude of an effect rather than for identifying the layer at which the effect appears. A framing condition may cause the model to sound more cautious, more empathetic, or more virtue-oriented, but it is often unclear whether the frame has changed the model's exposed first-pass choice or simply the rhetorical form of its explanation. In moral evaluation settings, this distinction is especially important. A prompt that moves explanation language without moving first-pass choice can still look like a substantial moral shift if the evaluation focuses on the full textual response. For that reason, the present paper differs from standard persona or value-framing work in two ways.

First, we compare a Christian heart-focused frame against a matched secular motive-focused control rather than against only an unframed baseline. This is crucial because it separates specifically Christian lexical framing from a more generic emphasis on motive and character. Second, we use pre- versus post-judgment timing to distinguish judgment-layer from explanation-layer effects. The `christian_post` condition is particularly diagnostic: because the Christian frame appears only after `J1` is already on record, any later shift is better interpreted as a post-hoc explanatory effect than as a broad first-pass judgment rewrite. In that sense, the paper contributes to the prompt-framing literature by adding a stage-separated causal design rather than merely another prompt variant.

More broadly, our results speak to a growing concern in prompt-based evaluation: not all prompt effects should be interpreted in the same way. Some interventions may genuinely alter exposed task behavior, while others primarily alter tone, rationale structure, or the vocabulary used to justify a stable answer. The present study suggests that moral prompting should be read through this lens. Prompt sensitivity is not just about whether the output changes, but also about which layer of the output changes.

## Moral Psychology, Social Intuitionism, and Post-Hoc Reasoning

The third background line comes from moral psychology, especially work associated with intuitionist accounts of moral judgment [Haidt, 2001; moral psychology review]. In the Social Intuitionist Model (SIM), moral judgment is often produced quickly and intuitively, while explicit reasoning frequently serves a justificatory or socially communicative role after the fact. Whether or not one accepts the full strength of this account for human cognition, it offers a valuable conceptual distinction between judgment and justification. That distinction is highly relevant for LLM evaluation because many model outputs are judged in settings where the rationale is treated as transparent evidence of the decision process.

We do not import SIM into LLM research as a literal theory of model internals. Models do not need to possess human intuitions, affective systems, or social reputational motives for SIM to be analytically useful here. Instead, SIM provides an operational vocabulary for a common ambiguity in LLM evaluation: a model may expose one judgment and then produce a rationale that sounds richer, more principled, or more value-laden than the judgment process itself warrants. In this setting, the analogy is about observable structure rather than inner psychology. A staged prompt design can therefore test a SIM-style question without claiming that LLMs are miniature human moral agents.

This moral-psychological background also highlights why motive-sensitive framing is a strong probe. If a frame emphasizes heart, intention, or inner posture, it may influence the model's immediate heart-level judgment, but it may also simply offer a richer vocabulary for retrospective justification. The present paper is built to separate these possibilities. In that sense, it contributes to the interface between moral psychology and NLP by translating a theory-level distinction into an experimentally tractable LLM benchmark design.

Existing work at this intersection has often invoked human moral theories to interpret model behavior, but less often to structure the response process itself. Our contribution is to use a moral-psychological model not only as an interpretive gloss but as a design principle. The resulting question is sharper than “does this frame change the answer?” It becomes: “does this frame change the first-pass exposed judgment, the post-hoc explanation, or both?” That distinction is the core gap this paper addresses.

## Gap Statement

Taken together, these three literatures leave a clear opening. Benchmark work provides realistic moral scenarios but often conflates judgment and explanation. Prompt-framing work shows that model outputs are steerable but often does not isolate which layer of the response has changed. Moral-psychological theory offers a distinction between judgment and post-hoc reasoning, but it has not been widely operationalized in LLM benchmark design. Our paper occupies this gap by combining an everyday moral benchmark, a matched Christian-versus-secular framing contrast, and a staged response structure that can separate first-pass exposed judgment from later explanation.

We therefore position this study not as a broad replacement for existing moral evaluation benchmarks, nor as a theological intervention in AI ethics, but as a mechanism-oriented extension of current LLM evaluation practice. The key novelty is not simply the use of Christian framing. It is the use of Christian framing to test whether prompt effects in LLM morality primarily appear at the level of first-pass judgment or at the level of post-hoc explanation.
