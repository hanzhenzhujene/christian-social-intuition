# Discussion Draft

The main supported contribution of the paper is mechanism separation. The staged design shows that explanation language moves more readily than first-pass exposed judgment, and that this difference matters for how prompt effects should be interpreted in LLM moral evaluation.

This result argues against naive readings of bundled judgment-plus-rationale outputs. A prompt can change explanation language substantially relative to baseline while leaving first-pass exposed choice largely stable. If evaluation collapses these layers into a single answer, explanation-layer movement can be misread as deeper judgment change.

The completed evidence is more consistent with stage dissociation and at least partial rhetorical uptake than with a strong uniquely Christian semantic advantage. In the 7B model, Christian framing preserves only a modest Christian-over-secular first-pass heart increment. But once the matched secular motive-focused control and lexical-echo control are applied, the explanation-layer evidence weakens sharply. The smaller 0.5B model narrows the story further rather than stabilizing it.

This makes the same-family comparison informative even though it is weaker than the earlier narrative. The point is not that the smaller model ``fails'' in some dramatic sense. The point is that the Christian-specific story is scale-sensitive within one family, which is more honest and more interpretable than claiming a robust architecture-level advantage.

Stronger explanation claims still await human annotation and fuller semantic validation. The paper can already make a clean mechanism claim, but it should stop short of final conclusions about judgment–explanation consistency or robust Christian-specific explanation quality.
