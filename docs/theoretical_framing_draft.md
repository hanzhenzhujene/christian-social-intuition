# Theoretical Framing Draft

## The Social Intuitionist Model as an Operational Lens

The central theoretical reference point for this paper is Haidt's Social Intuitionist Model (SIM) [Haidt, 2001]. In its broadest form, SIM argues that moral judgment is often produced quickly and intuitively, while explicit reasoning frequently serves a justificatory role after the judgment is already in place. In human moral psychology, this claim is tied to a wider view about moral persuasion, social communication, and the limits of deliberative self-report. We do not attempt to transfer that full psychological architecture directly to LLMs. Instead, we use SIM as an operational lens for thinking about a recurrent ambiguity in prompt-based LLM evaluation: when a model produces a moral answer and an accompanying rationale, the rationale is often treated as if it transparently reveals the process by which the judgment was reached. SIM gives us a principled reason to question that assumption.

The usefulness of SIM in the present setting comes from its separation of judgment and justification. Standard LLM evaluation pipelines often collapse those two layers. A model is presented with a scenario, returns a decision plus a rationale, and the combined output is treated as a single unit of moral reasoning. That is a convenient format for benchmarking, but it makes an important interpretive distinction difficult to observe. If a prompt changes the explanation while leaving the exposed initial choice intact, the resulting response may still look substantially different. Conversely, if the prompt changes a first-pass exposed judgment, that shift may be obscured when the explanation is scored alongside it as part of one answer. SIM therefore helps us identify a measurement problem that is already latent in current LLM moral prompting practice.

Our goal is not to claim that LLMs have human-like intuitions, emotions, or socially situated post-hoc defenses. The present paper does not infer hidden internal states from external text. Instead, we operationalize a simpler observable distinction: first-pass exposed judgment versus subsequent explanation. In this design, “intuition” is shorthand for the model's initial exposed judgment under conditions that disallow explanation, not for any strong claim about a fast, affective inner mechanism. This distinction matters because the paper's contribution rests on a behavioral dissociation. If a framing manipulation changes explanation language more strongly than first-pass exposed judgment, then the effect is better understood as an explanation-layer intervention than as a broad rewrite of exposed moral choice.

## Mapping SIM onto the Staged Design

The staged prompt structure gives SIM a concrete experimental analogue. `J1` collects a first-pass forced-choice judgment with no explanation. `E` then asks the model to explain its earlier judgment without revising it, and `J2` records whether any judgment changes after explanation and framing have had time to operate on the response sequence. The mapping is intentionally modest. `J1` is not treated as a direct measurement of latent intuition in the human sense. `E` is not treated as pure conscious confabulation. And `J2` is not assumed to reveal a stable reflective equilibrium. Rather, the three stages provide a way to observe whether prompt effects distribute differently across exposed judgment, explicit explanation, and post-explanation revision.

This mapping matters because it makes several competing interpretations testable. If a framing manipulation changes `J1`, then the manipulation is affecting first-pass exposed judgment. If it does not change `J1` but does change `E`, then the manipulation is affecting explanation-layer output more than initial judgment. If it changes `J2` after explanation, then the manipulation may support downstream reconsideration or destabilization even when the initial choice was already recorded. These are distinct empirical possibilities, and the point of the design is precisely to keep them from collapsing into a single moral score.

SIM is especially useful here because it suggests that post-hoc explanation may carry a different causal signature from first-pass judgment. The paper's cleanest test of this idea is the `christian_post` condition. In that condition, the Christian frame appears only after `J1` is already fixed. This means any subsequent shift in explanation cannot be attributed to a direct first-pass judgment effect. If `christian_post` leaves `J1` unchanged while increasing heart-focused or Christianized explanation language, that is exactly the kind of layered pattern SIM makes theoretically salient. The value of the framework is therefore not that it proves the model is intuitionist in a human sense, but that it provides a disciplined way to interpret stage-specific prompt effects.

## Why Christian Framing Is Theoretically Informative

Christian heart-focused framing is useful in this framework because it can, in principle, act at two different levels. First, it may alter what the model treats as morally central in first-pass judgment, especially at the level of motive, character, and inner posture. If so, we should see movement in `J1`, especially in `heart_worse`. Second, it may instead function as a repertoire of post-hoc moral vocabulary, allowing the model to redescribe a stable judgment in terms of heart, selfishness, temptation, and inner character. If so, we should see stronger effects in `E` than in `J1`.

The matched secular motive-focused control is important because it helps narrow the theoretical interpretation. Without that control, a Christian-versus-baseline difference could be explained by any generic shift toward motive salience. The control lets us ask a more precise question: does Christian wording add anything beyond a non-religious emphasis on motive, intention, and inner posture? This makes the theoretical contrast sharper. The relevant comparison is not merely religion versus no religion, but Christian heart-language versus a closely matched motive frame that removes explicitly Christian lexical cues.

This is why the paper's central theoretical claim is deliberately moderate. The data do not support a simple “Christian framing changes moral judgment” conclusion. Nor do they support the claim that Christian framing is purely decorative. Instead, the design allows us to ask where the Christian effect is strongest. In the completed runs, that question produces a differentiated answer: on the main model, Christian framing shows only a modest extra movement over the secular motive control at the level of first-pass heart judgment, but a clearer post-hoc effect in explanation language. That pattern is better described as a layered mechanism than as a unitary moral shift.

## What This Framework Lets Us Claim

Using SIM as an operational framework changes the type of claim this paper can responsibly make. The strongest claim is not that the models reason like humans, nor that Christian prompting reveals their “true” moral commitments. The strongest claim is that prompt effects in LLM moral evaluation can separate across stages in a way that is theoretically meaningful. A prompt may affect first-pass exposed judgment somewhat, post-hoc explanation more strongly, or both in different proportions. Once this possibility is visible, the common practice of evaluating a single bundled moral response becomes harder to defend as a complete measure of prompt-sensitive moral reasoning.

This framework also helps discipline the paper's negative claims. If the main signal had appeared only in `J1`, then the paper would have supported a stronger judgment-rewrite interpretation. If it had appeared only in `E`, then the paper would have supported a stronger post-hoc rationalization interpretation. Instead, the completed results support an intermediate position. Motive-focused pre-framing can move some heart-level first-pass judgments, but Christian wording has its most distinctive effect at the explanation layer. That is exactly the kind of layered empirical result for which SIM is useful: it gives us a vocabulary for distinguishing exposed judgment from later justification without turning the analysis into an all-or-nothing psychological story.
